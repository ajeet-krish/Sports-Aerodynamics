Background Theory

The governing equations and physical concepts behind every simulation

The Navier-Stokes Equations

The fundamental law of fluid motion. All CFD simulations in this project solve the incompressible Navier-Stokes equations:

\[ \frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial t} + (\mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla)\mathbf{u} = -\frac{1}{\rho}\nabla p + \nu \nabla^2 \mathbf{u} \]

\[ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} = 0 \]

Symbol Quantity Units Role
\(\mathbf{u}\) Velocity vector m/s Primary variable — solved on a staggered (MAC) grid
\(p\) Pressure Pa Enforces incompressibility via the pressure-Poisson equation
\(\rho\) Density kg/m³ ~1.2 kg/m³ for air at sea level
\(\nu\) Kinematic viscosity m²/s ~1.5 × 10⁻⁵ m²/s for air
\(\nabla p\) Pressure gradient Pa/m Drives flow from high to low pressure
\(\nu \nabla^2 \mathbf{u}\) Viscous diffusion m/s² Dissipates momentum, smooths velocity gradients

Physical Interpretation of Each Term

  • \(\partial \mathbf{u} / \partial t\) — Local acceleration: how velocity changes at a fixed point in space.
  • \((\mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla)\mathbf{u}\) — Advection (convection): fluid momentum carried along by its own motion. This is the non-linear term that makes turbulence possible.
  • \(-(1/\rho) \nabla p\) — Pressure gradient force: fluid moves from regions of high pressure to low pressure. In a free kick, the pressure difference across the ball is what curves its trajectory.
  • \(\nu \nabla^2 \mathbf{u}\) — Viscous diffusion: friction between adjacent fluid layers. Responsible for boundary layers, drag, and the no-slip condition at solid surfaces.
  • \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} = 0\) — Incompressibility constraint: the volume of a fluid element does not change. Valid for low-speed air flow (Mach < 0.3).

Velocity & the Staggered Grid

Why we store velocity components at different locations.

In ΦFlow, the velocity field is stored on a staggered (MAC) grid:

  • x-velocity components live on vertical cell faces
  • y-velocity components live on horizontal cell faces
  • Pressure and scalar fields live at cell centres

This arrangement prevents the “checkerboard” pressure decoupling that plagues collocated grids. The pressure gradient at a face is naturally computed from the pressure difference between adjacent cell centres.

Centered Grid for Visualisation

For plotting, we interpolate the staggered velocity to cell centres:

\[ \mathbf{u}_{\text{centered}} = \mathbf{v} \ @ \ \text{CenteredGrid}(\dots) \]

This allows streamlines and quiver plots on a regular grid, matching the visualisation grid to the pressure grid.

The Reynolds Number

The single most important dimensionless group in fluid dynamics:

\[ Re = \frac{UL}{\nu} \]

Where \(U\) is a characteristic velocity, \(L\) is a characteristic length, and \(\nu\) is the kinematic viscosity. The Reynolds number quantifies the ratio of inertial forces to viscous forces:

  • Low Re (< 1): Viscous forces dominate. Flow is laminar, smooth, and reversible (Stokes flow).
  • Intermediate Re (10² – 10⁵): Boundary layers form; flow may separate behind bluff bodies, creating wakes.
  • High Re (> 10⁵): Inertia dominates. Boundary layers transition to turbulent; wakes are chaotic.

Reynolds Number in This Project

Simulation \(U\) \(L\) \(Re\) Flow regime
Shot aerodynamics (cylinder) 1.0 m/s 0.6 m ~4 × 10⁴ Subcritical — laminar BL, turbulent wake
Tactical positioning (player) 10.0 m/s 0.3 m ~2 × 10⁵ Critical — BL transition possible
Real soccer ball (full scale) 25 m/s 0.22 m ~3.5 × 10⁵ Supercritical — drag crisis

Pressure & the Poisson Equation

How the solver enforces incompressibility. Taking the divergence of the momentum equation and applying the incompressibility constraint yields the pressure-Poisson equation:

\[ \nabla^2 p = -\rho \nabla \cdot [(\mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla)\mathbf{u}] \]

In ΦFlow, this is solved with a conjugate-gradient (CG) iteration at each time step. The pressure field is the Lagrange multiplier that enforces the divergence-free constraint.

Physical Meaning

  • High pressure on the windward side of an obstacle — fluid stagnates, converting kinetic energy to pressure energy.
  • Low pressure in the wake — separated flow creates a suction zone that contributes the majority of pressure drag.
  • Pressure gradient across a rotating cylinder (Magnus effect) generates lift perpendicular to the flow.

Why Pressure Matters for a Soccer Ball

The trajectory of a spinning ball is determined almost entirely by the pressure field:

  • The Magnus force is \(F_L \propto \Delta p \times A\), where \(\Delta p\) is the pressure difference between opposite sides of the ball.
  • Drag is \(F_D = \frac{1}{2} \rho U^2 C_D A\), with \(C_D\) determined largely by the pressure recovery (or lack thereof) on the rear surface.
  • Knuckleball effect arises from asymmetric vortex shedding creating fluctuating pressure fields that are hard for a goalkeeper to read.

Streamlines, Vorticity & the Wake

Visualising flow structure.

Streamlines

A streamline is a curve everywhere tangent to the instantaneous velocity field. They show the direction of flow at every point. In our simulations, streamlines are coloured by velocity magnitude to reveal fast-moving channels and stagnant recirculation zones.

Vorticity

Vorticity \(\omega = \nabla \times \mathbf{u}\) measures the local rotation of a fluid element. In 2D:

\[ \omega = \frac{\partial v}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} \]

  • Positive vorticity = counter-clockwise rotation
  • Negative vorticity = clockwise rotation
  • A vortex street (alternating sign) behind a bluff body is the hallmark of von Kármán vortex shedding.

The Wake

The wake is the region of retarded flow behind an obstacle. Its structure determines drag:

  • Narrow wake = efficient pressure recovery, low drag (streamlined body).
  • Wide, unsteady wake = large pressure deficit, high drag (bluff body).
  • Spin deflects the wake, creating a pressure imbalance that produces lift (Magnus effect).

Boundary Layers & Separation

Where drag is born. When a fluid flows past a solid surface, the no-slip condition requires the velocity to be zero at the wall. The thin region where velocity transitions from zero to the free-stream value is the boundary layer.

As the flow moves around a curved surface (like a cylinder or a soccer ball), the boundary layer experiences an adverse pressure gradient on the rear side. If this gradient is strong enough, the boundary layer separates — it detaches from the surface, forming a wake.

Key insight: Spinning the ball shifts the separation point asymmetrically. On the side moving with the flow, the boundary layer is energised and separates later. On the opposing side, it separates earlier. This asymmetric wake produces the Magnus lift that curls free kicks.

Numerical Methods in ΦFlow

How the equations are discretised and solved.

Semi-Lagrangian Advection

Instead of tracking fluid particles explicitly, semi-Lagrangian advection traces back along streamlines to sample the velocity field at an earlier time. This allows larger time steps than purely Eulerian schemes and is unconditionally stable for the advection term.

Conjugate-Gradient Pressure Solve

At each time step, after advection, the velocity field is generally not divergence-free. The pressure-Poisson equation is solved with an iterative CG method to find the pressure correction that projects the velocity onto the divergence-free subspace.

Obstacle Boundary Conditions

Solid obstacles (cylinders, rectangular players) are embedded in the grid using ΦFlow’s Obstacle class. For rotating obstacles, the angular_velocity parameter imposes a tangential velocity on the obstacle surface, modelling the no-slip condition on a spinning body.

Continuum Mechanics & Transport Phenomena

The second modelling paradigm in this project. While the shot aerodynamics and tactical positioning modules solve the full Navier-Stokes equations, the stress fields and team heatmaps use a different approach: Gaussian influence superposition.

Gaussian Influence Fields

Each player is modelled as a 2D Gaussian:

\[ \phi(x, y) = \sum_{i} \exp\left[ -\frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{(x - x_i)^2}{\sigma_x^2} + \frac{(y - y_i)^2}{\sigma_y^2} \right) \right] \]

The total influence field is the sum over all 7 (stress) or 11 (heatmap) players. For the stress field, the Gaussian is stretched in the direction of player velocity, creating an anisotropic influence that reflects momentum.

Darcy Permeability Analogy

In the team heatmaps, regions of low influence (below a threshold) represent open space — passing lanes for the attacking team. The fraction of the defensive half where influence is below threshold is the permeability:

  • High permeability (e.g. 46% in a spread 4-2-3-1) = porous defence, many passing channels open.
  • Low permeability (e.g. 7% in a compact 5-3-2) = choked defence, passing lanes constricted.

Von Mises Yield Criterion

In the stress field, the attack-defence difference \(\sigma = \tanh((\phi_{\text{attack}} - \phi_{\text{defence}}) / 3.0)\) defines a continuum stress. Local maxima exceeding a threshold (\(\sigma > 0.4\)) are identified as yield points — structural breaches where the attacking force has locally exceeded the defensive block’s strength.
